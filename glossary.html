Civic Terms Glossary

Clear definitions for common election and government terms.

Common terms, clearer definitions.

This glossary explains common terms you may see while researching elections, reading a ballot, or learning how government works. Definitions are general. Exact rules can vary by state, county, city, or election.

Election terms Voting terms Ballot terms Political and election system terms Government terms
01

Election terms

Election

A process where voters choose candidates for public office or vote directly on public questions.

Candidate

A person running for elected office.

Office

A specific government position, such as mayor, governor, senator, judge, or school board member.

Incumbent

A person who currently holds the office they are running for again.

Term

The length of time an elected official serves in office before the next election for that position.

Primary election

An election that helps decide which candidates move forward to the general election.

General election

The main election where voters choose who will hold office.

Special election

An election held outside the normal election schedule, often because an office became vacant.

Midterm election

An election that happens halfway through a president’s four-year term.

Local election

An election for city, town, county, school district, or other community-level offices.

Municipal election

An election for city, town, or borough offices, such as mayor or city council.

02

Voting terms

Voter registration

The process of signing up so your state or local election office knows you are eligible to vote.

Registration deadline

The last day to register to vote before an election.

Polling place

The location where a voter can vote in person.

Precinct

A smaller voting area within a city, county, or district. Your precinct can affect what appears on your ballot.

Sample ballot

A preview of what will appear on your actual ballot.

Absentee ballot

A ballot used by a voter who cannot or does not vote in person on Election Day, depending on state rules.

Mail ballot

A ballot that is sent to a voter and returned by mail or another approved method.

Early voting

Voting in person before Election Day, if allowed in your state.

Provisional ballot

A ballot used when there is a question about a voter’s eligibility. Election officials review it before deciding whether it can be counted.

Write-in candidate

A candidate whose name is not printed on the ballot, but whose name voters may be able to write in.

Undervote

When a voter leaves a race or question blank on their ballot.

Overvote

When a voter selects more candidates than allowed in a race. This can prevent that race from being counted.

03

Ballot terms

Ballot

The official form voters use to make choices in an election.

Candidate race

A section of the ballot where voters choose a person for public office.

Ballot measure

A question voters decide directly, often about a law, tax, bond, amendment, or public policy issue.

Referendum

A type of ballot question where voters approve or reject a law or proposal.

Initiative

A process that allows citizens to propose a law or policy and place it on the ballot, depending on state rules.

Constitutional amendment

A change to a constitution. Some amendments must be approved by voters.

Bond measure

A ballot question asking voters whether a government can borrow money for a specific project or purpose.

Retention vote

A vote asking whether a judge should stay in office for another term.

Judicial election

An election involving judges or justices.

Local question

A ballot question that affects a specific city, county, school district, or community.

04

Political and election system terms

Partisan

Connected to a political party.

Nonpartisan

Not officially connected to a political party.

Political party

An organization of people with shared political goals that supports candidates for office.

Independent candidate

A candidate who is not running as the nominee of a major political party.

Third-party candidate

A candidate running with a political party other than the two major parties.

Plurality

When the candidate with the most votes wins, even if they do not receive more than half of all votes.

Majority

More than half of the votes.

Runoff election

A second election held when no candidate wins enough votes in the first election.

Ranked-choice voting

A voting system where voters rank candidates in order of preference.

Certification

The official process of confirming final election results.

05

Government terms

Government

The system of institutions, offices, and officials that make, carry out, and interpret laws.

Local government

Government at the city, town, county, school district, or community level.

State government

Government that makes and carries out laws for an entire state.

Federal government

The national government of the United States.

Executive branch

The branch of government responsible for carrying out and enforcing laws.

Legislative branch

The branch of government responsible for making laws.

Judicial branch

The branch of government responsible for interpreting and applying laws in court cases.

District

A geographic area represented by an elected official.

Representative

An elected official chosen to speak and vote on behalf of people in a district.

Legislature

A lawmaking body, such as Congress or a state legislature.

Ordinance

A local law passed by a city, town, or county government.

Budget

A plan for how government money will be collected and spent.

Public hearing

A public meeting where people can give input before a government decision is made.

Public comment

A chance for members of the public to share opinions, concerns, or information with government officials.

Agenda

A list of topics or items planned for discussion at a public meeting.

Quorum

The minimum number of members who must be present for a government body to officially conduct business.
