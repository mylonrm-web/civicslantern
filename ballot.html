How to Read a Ballot

Understand candidate races, ballot measures, judicial elections, retention votes, and local questions before you vote.

A ballot can include more than candidate names.

When people think about voting, they often imagine choosing between candidates for president, governor, or Congress. But a real ballot can include much more than that: local offices, judges, ballot measures, constitutional amendments, tax questions, retention votes, and other public decisions.

That can feel overwhelming, especially if you are seeing some of those offices or questions for the first time. This guide breaks down the common parts of a ballot so you can understand what you are looking at before you vote.

The basic idea

A ballot is the official form voters use to make choices in an election.

Depending on where you live and what election is happening, your ballot may ask you to choose candidates for public office, vote yes or no on ballot questions, decide whether judges should stay in office, or weigh in on local issues.

Not every ballot looks the same. Ballots vary by state, county, city, school district, precinct, and election year. Two people in the same state may have different ballots if they live in different districts.

01

Candidate races

Candidate races are the parts of the ballot where voters choose a person for a public office.

These races may include offices like:

  • President
  • U.S. senator
  • U.S. representative
  • Governor
  • State senator
  • State representative
  • Mayor
  • City council member
  • County commissioner
  • School board member
  • Judge
  • Sheriff
  • District attorney

When reading a candidate race, start by identifying the office. Before comparing candidates, it helps to understand what the job actually does. A candidate running for school board, for example, is running for a very different role than a candidate running for U.S. Senate.

Helpful questions to ask

  • What office is this race for?
  • What level of government does this office belong to?
  • What responsibilities does this office have?
  • How long is the term?
  • How many candidates can voters select?
  • Is this a partisan or nonpartisan race?
  • Where can I find official or reliable information about the candidates?

Some races ask voters to choose one candidate. Others may say something like “Vote for not more than two,” which means voters may choose up to that number of candidates.

02

Party labels

Some ballots list a candidate’s political party next to their name. Other races may be nonpartisan, meaning candidates appear without party labels.

A party label can give voters some context, but it does not explain everything about a candidate. Candidates from the same party can still have different priorities, backgrounds, experience, and policy positions.

For nonpartisan races, like some school board, judicial, or local races, voters may need to do more research because the ballot may not include party information.

A useful approach is to look at the office first, then research the candidate’s background, official website, public statements, experience, and credible nonpartisan sources.

03

Ballot measures

A ballot measure is a question voters decide directly.

Instead of choosing between candidates, voters may be asked to vote “yes” or “no” on a proposed law, constitutional amendment, bond, tax change, local rule, or public policy question.

Ballot measures can be confusing because they are sometimes written in formal legal language. A “yes” vote usually supports the proposed change, while a “no” vote usually opposes it, but the wording can still be tricky, so it is important to read carefully.

When reading a ballot measure, ask:

  • What would change if this passes?
  • What would stay the same if it fails?
  • Who would be affected?
  • Is this a state issue or a local issue?
  • Is it about a law, tax, bond, budget, charter, or constitutional change?
  • Where can I find the official explanation or full text?
04

Local questions

Local questions are ballot items that affect a specific city, county, school district, or community.

These may involve issues like:

  • School funding
  • Local taxes
  • Public services
  • Bonds for buildings or infrastructure
  • City or county charter changes
  • Public transportation
  • Emergency services
  • Libraries, parks, or community programs

Local questions may not get as much attention as national races, but they can have a direct effect on daily life. They may influence local budgets, schools, roads, buildings, services, or community rules.

When researching a local question, it helps to check your county election office, local government website, school district website, or official voter guide.

05

Judicial elections

Judicial elections are races involving judges.

Judges do not have the same role as legislators or executives. They are not elected to write laws or run agencies. Their job is to interpret and apply the law in court cases.

Depending on where you live, judicial races may be partisan, nonpartisan, appointed, elected, or handled through retention elections. Some ballots may include local judges, state court judges, appellate judges, or state supreme court justices.

When reading a judicial race, ask:

  • What court is this judge running for?
  • What kinds of cases does that court hear?
  • Is this a regular election or a retention vote?
  • Is the race partisan or nonpartisan?
  • What experience does the candidate have?
  • Are there official judicial evaluations or nonpartisan legal resources available?

Judicial races can be harder to research because candidates may avoid taking public positions on issues that could come before the court. In those cases, voters may look at experience, legal background, courtroom conduct, endorsements from legal organizations, public records, and official judicial ratings where available.

06

Retention votes

A retention vote asks voters whether a judge should stay in office. Instead of choosing between multiple candidates, the ballot may ask something like:

“Shall Judge [Name] be retained for another term?”

Voters usually choose “yes” or “no.”

When researching a retention vote, look for:

  • What court the judge serves on
  • How long the judge has served
  • Professional background
  • Judicial evaluations, if available
  • Publicly available records or opinions
  • Official court or election information
07

Instructions on the ballot

Every ballot includes instructions. These instructions matter.

They may tell you:

  1. How many candidates you can choose
  2. Whether you can vote for one or more candidates
  3. How to mark your ballot
  4. What to do if you make a mistake
  5. Whether write-in candidates are allowed
  6. How to review your choices before submitting

One of the easiest mistakes to make is missing a phrase like “Vote for one” or “Vote for not more than three.” If a race allows multiple selections, the ballot should say so.

08

You do not have to vote on every item

In many elections, voters are allowed to leave some races or questions blank. This is sometimes called an undervote. For example, if you feel prepared to vote in some races but not others, you may still be able to submit your ballot with only some sections completed.

Before you vote

How to prepare before voting

  1. Find your sample ballot from your official state or county election office.
  2. Make a list of every race and question on it.
  3. Identify what each office actually does.
  4. Research candidates using official campaign pages, government pages, and nonpartisan sources.
  5. Read ballot measures or local questions carefully.
  6. Check whether there are official explanations or voter guides.
  7. Save notes so you remember what you learned before voting.
