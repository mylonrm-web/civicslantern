What Different Offices Actually Do

It is hard to evaluate candidates if you do not know what job they are running for.

Not every office has the same kind of power.

A ballot can include a long list of offices, from mayor, governor, senator, representative, judge, school board member, county commissioner, district attorney, sheriff, and more. Some of these titles may sound familiar, but it is not always obvious what each person actually has the power to do.

This guide breaks down common elected offices in plain language, so you can better understand what responsibilities come with each role before researching the candidates running for them.

Quick reminder

Government offices can work differently depending on where you live. A mayor in one city may have different powers than a mayor in another city. A sheriff’s duties may look different from state to state. Judges may be elected in some places and appointed in others.

The basic idea

Think of elected offices as jobs within government.

When voters choose someone for an office, they are choosing who will hold certain responsibilities for a set period of time. Some offices make laws. Some enforce laws. Some manage budgets. Some oversee schools. Some decide court cases. Some represent a district, while others represent an entire city, county, state, or country.

Closest to home

Local offices

Local offices often affect the services, rules, and decisions people experience most directly in their communities.

City or town

Mayor

A mayor is usually a city or town’s top elected executive. Mayors may help propose budgets, represent the city publicly, respond to emergencies, and work with the city council on local policy.

The exact power of a mayor depends heavily on the city’s form of government. Local governments can be structured in different ways, such as mayor-council and council-manager systems.

City or town

City council member

A city council member helps make decisions for a city, town, or borough. City councils usually pass local laws, approve budgets, set priorities, regulate land use, and oversee local programs.

A city council may have power over tax rates, ordinances, zoning, public health and safety rules, contracts, and long-term community goals.

School district

School board member

A school board member helps govern a public school district. School boards usually make decisions about district policy, budgets, the superintendent, curriculum priorities, school facilities, and long-term goals.

School boards are one of the most direct ways voters influence public education.

County

County commissioner or council member

A county commissioner or county council member helps govern county-level services. Counties often handle responsibilities that cross city or town lines, like county budgets, courts, public health, emergency services, roads, human services, elections administration, and land records.

They are often responsible for budgets, policy, and oversight.

County

Sheriff

A sheriff is usually a county-level law enforcement official. Depending on the state and county, a sheriff’s office may run a jail, provide courtroom security, serve court documents, transport people in custody, enforce warrants, or provide law enforcement services in certain areas.

Criminal justice

District attorney

A district attorney, county attorney, or local prosecutor represents the government in criminal cases. This office may decide whether to bring charges, what charges to pursue, how to handle plea agreements, and how to manage prosecution priorities.

Courts

Local judge

A local judge handles cases in a court. Depending on the court, judges may hear criminal cases, civil disputes, traffic cases, family matters, landlord-tenant issues, or other legal conflicts. Courts apply the law to specific disputes.

Across the state

State offices

State officials make decisions about statewide laws, budgets, agencies, courts, and public programs.

Executive branch

Governor

A governor is the top elected executive of a state. Governors oversee the state executive branch, help carry out state laws, propose budgets, respond to emergencies, appoint certain officials, and often have the power to sign or veto bills passed by the state legislature.

Executive branch

Lieutenant governor

A lieutenant governor is usually the second-highest executive official in a state. The role varies a lot by state. In some states, the lieutenant governor has major responsibilities. In others, the role is more limited.

Often, the lieutenant governor may become governor if the governor can no longer serve. In some states, they may also preside over the state senate, lead special initiatives, or support the governor’s agenda.

State legislature

State senator

A state senator is a member of the state legislature. State senators help write, debate, amend, and vote on state laws. They also represent people in a specific district.

State legislatures make decisions about issues like education, healthcare, transportation, taxes, criminal justice, voting rules, environmental policy, and state budgets. State legislatures also oversee state agencies and how laws are being carried out.

State legislature

State representative

A state representative, delegate, or assembly member also serves in the state legislature. Like state senators, they help create state laws, vote on budgets, serve on committees, and represent a district.

Many states have two legislative chambers, similar to the federal government. State legislators make laws for the state and represent people in their districts.

Legal officer

State attorney general

A state attorney general is usually the state’s top legal officer. The attorney general may represent the state in court, issue legal opinions, enforce certain state laws, protect consumers, and oversee legal action involving the state government.

Courts

State judge

A state judge handles legal cases in the state court system. State courts handle most legal cases in the United States, including many criminal, civil, family, and probate matters.

Some state judges are elected, some are appointed, and some face retention elections depending on the state and court level.

National government

Federal offices

Federal officials make decisions about national laws, federal agencies, courts, spending, and countrywide policy.

Executive branch

President

The president leads the federal executive branch. The president is responsible for implementing and enforcing laws passed by Congress, appointing leaders of federal agencies, serving as commander-in-chief of the armed forces, and representing the United States in national and international matters.

The president can also sign or veto bills passed by Congress, and nominates federal judges and other officials, with Senate confirmation.

Legislative branch

U.S. senator

A U.S. senator represents an entire state in the United States Senate. Each state has two senators.

Senators help write and vote on federal laws, approve or reject many presidential nominees, vote on treaties, serve on committees, and conduct oversight of the federal government. The Senate also holds impeachment trials after the House impeaches a federal official.

Legislative branch

U.S. representative

A U.S. representative serves in the United States House of Representatives and represents a congressional district. Representatives help make and pass federal laws.

The House has 435 voting representatives, with seats divided among the states based on population.

Judicial branch

Federal judge

A federal judge hears cases in the federal court system. Federal courts deal with issues involving federal law, the Constitution, disputes between states, federal crimes, bankruptcy, and other matters assigned to federal courts.

U.S. district courts are federal trial courts, meaning they resolve disputes by determining facts and applying the law to those facts.

Why it matters

A candidate’s promises should match the office they are running for.

For example, a school board candidate may have influence over district policies, but they cannot single-handedly change federal law. A mayor may shape city priorities, but they usually do not control state education funding. A U.S. senator can vote on federal laws, but they do not directly run your local police department.

Questions to ask

  • What power does this office actually have?
  • What decisions would this person be responsible for?
  • Are their campaign promises connected to the job?
  • What level of government does this issue belong to?
  • Where can I check the official duties of this office?
