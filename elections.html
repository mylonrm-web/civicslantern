How Elections Work

An election is a process where voters choose candidates for public office or vote directly on certain questions, laws, or changes. Elections can happen at the local, state, or federal level, and each one affects a different part of civic life.

Some elections decide national leadership. Others decide who runs your school district, who manages your town or city, who represents your state, or how local tax money is used. Even smaller elections can have a major impact on daily life.

A good place to start is knowing what level of government an election involves, what office or question is on the ballot, and where to find official information before you vote.

The basic idea

At its simplest, an election asks voters to make a choice.

That choice might be between candidates running for an office, such as mayor, governor, senator, or school board member. It might also be about a ballot measure, which is a question voters decide directly.

Elections are one of the main ways people participate in government. Voting does not solve every civic problem by itself, but it is one way communities decide who has power, what policies move forward, and what priorities matter the most to them.

Different levels of elections

Elections can happen at different levels of government. Each level has different responsibilities, and each one can affect public life in a different way.

Local elections

Local elections choose leaders for cities, towns, counties, school districts, courts, and many other community offices.

These elections may decide positions such as mayor, city council member, county commissioner, school board member, local judge, district attorney, or sheriff.

Local elections often affect schools, libraries, roads, policing, housing rules, public transportation, parks, and local budgets.

State elections

State elections choose leaders who make decisions for an entire state.

These elections may decide positions such as governor, lieutenant governor, state senator, state representative, attorney general, state judges, or other statewide offices.

State governments make decisions about education, healthcare, voting rules, transportation, criminal justice, taxes, environmental policy, and state budgets.

Federal elections

Federal elections choose leaders who make decisions for the country as a whole.

These elections may include the president, U.S. senator, or U.S. representative.

Federal officials make decisions about national laws, federal spending, foreign policy, national defense, civil rights, immigration, and other countrywide issues.

Common types of elections

Elections do not all serve the same purpose. Some narrow down a list of candidates, some choose who will hold office, and some happen only when a position opens unexpectedly.

Primary elections

A primary election helps decide which candidates will move forward to the general election.

In many races, multiple candidates from the same political party may want to run for the same office. A primary narrows that field down. The rules for who can vote in a primary can vary by state and party, so voters should always check their state’s official election website.

General elections

A general election is the main election where voters choose who will hold office.

This is usually when candidates from different parties, and sometimes independent or third-party candidates, appear on the ballot together. The winner of the general election usually takes office after the election is certified and the term begins.

Midterm elections

Midterm elections happen halfway through a president’s four-year term.

Even though the president is not on the ballot, midterms are still important. Voters may choose members of Congress, governors, state legislators, local officials, judges, and other offices, depending on the year and location.

Special elections

A special election happens outside the normal election schedule.

Special elections are often held when an office becomes vacant before the end of a term, such as when an official resigns, dies, or takes another position.

Local and municipal elections

Some cities, towns, counties, and school districts hold elections in years when there is no major national election.

These elections sometimes receive less attention, but they can have a direct impact on everyday life in a community.

How a candidate gets on the ballot

Before voters see a candidate’s name on a ballot, the candidate usually has to complete certain steps. The exact rules depend on the office and state, but the process may include:

  1. Declaring that they are running
  2. Filing official paperwork
  3. Collecting petition signatures
  4. Paying a filing fee
  5. Meeting eligibility requirements
  6. Winning a primary or nomination process

What happens before Election Day

Before an election, voters usually have time to learn about the candidates, offices, and ballot questions. This may include candidate announcements, campaign events, debates or public forums, voter registration deadlines, mail ballot or absentee ballot deadlines, early voting depending on the state, and publication of sample ballots or voter guides.

This is also when voters can research what will appear on their ballot before they vote.

What happens when people vote

Voting can happen in different ways depending on the state and election. Common voting options may include:

  1. Voting in person on Election Day
  2. Voting early in person
  3. Voting by mail
  4. Voting absentee

Not every option is available everywhere. Voting rules, deadlines, and ID requirements can vary by state.

How winners are decided

In many elections, the candidate with the most votes wins. This is often called a plurality system, meaning a candidate can win by receiving more votes than anyone else, even if they do not receive more than half of all votes.

Some elections use different systems, such as runoffs or ranked-choice voting. These systems work differently and are only used in certain places. After voting ends, election officials count and verify the results. The results may not be final immediately, especially if mail ballots, provisional ballots, or close races are involved in the election.

Why smaller elections matter

National elections often get the most attention, but local and state elections can shape daily life in very direct ways. Local officials may make decisions about schools, roads, zoning, policing, libraries, emergency services, and community budgets. State officials may decide laws and policies that affect education, healthcare, transportation, courts, and voting access.

Understanding elections means recognizing that civic participation does not happen just every four years. Many important decisions are made in elections that receive far less attention than the presidential race.
