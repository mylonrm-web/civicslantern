Understanding the Levels of Government

Civic issues usually have a “where” before they have a “who.”

Where is the decision being made?

When something affects your life, it is not always obvious which part of government is responsible for it. Some issues are handled locally. Some are decided at the state level. Others are controlled by the federal government. Many issues involve more than one level at the same time.

The basic idea

Local, state, and federal governments do not all do the same job. They each have different responsibilities, and they often interact with each other.

Local

Local government

Local government often handles decisions that affect your immediate community.

This can include things like schools, roads, libraries, public safety, zoning, local taxes, emergency services, parks, public meetings, and community programs.

Local government matters because it is often the easiest level to access. Meetings may be open to the public, officials may represent smaller communities, and decisions may affect places you interact with every day.

State

State government

State government makes decisions that apply across the entire state.

This can include education policy, state budgets, healthcare programs, transportation, criminal justice, voting rules, state courts, environmental rules, and many public services.

State government matters because it sets the foundation local governments have to work within. A school board may make district decisions, for example, but state law can shape funding, curriculum requirements, graduation rules, and testing policies.

Federal

Federal government

The federal government handles issues that affect the country as a whole.

This can include national defense, federal taxes, immigration, civil rights, federal courts, interstate commerce, federal programs, foreign policy, and national laws.

Federal government matters because it can set national standards, protect constitutional rights, fund major programs, and make decisions that apply across all states.

Shared responsibility

Sometimes more than one level is involved.

Many civic issues do not belong to only one level of government. Education can involve local school boards, state education departments, state legislatures, and federal funding or civil rights rules. Transportation can involve city streets, county roads, state highways, and federal infrastructure funding. Voting can involve local election offices, state election laws, and federal voting protections.

This is why civic issues can feel confusing. The same issue may have different decision-makers at different levels.

Education

Local school boards, state education departments, state legislatures, and federal rules or funding.

Transportation

City streets, county roads, state highways, and federal infrastructure funding.

Voting

Local election offices, state election laws, and federal voting protections.

How to figure out who is responsible

When you are trying to understand an issue, start with a few questions:

  • Is this issue happening in my school, town, city, county, state, or country?
  • Is the decision being made by a board, council, legislature, agency, court, or elected official?
  • Is this about a law, a budget, a policy, a service, or an individual case?
  • Does the issue affect only my community, the whole state, or the whole country?
  • Who has the power to approve, reject, fund, enforce, or change the decision?
